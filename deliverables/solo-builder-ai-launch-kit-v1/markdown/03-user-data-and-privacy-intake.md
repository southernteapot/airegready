# User Data and Privacy Intake

Purpose: Map what user, customer, client, buyer, or audience data touches your AI workflow before you launch or change it.

## When To Use It

Use this when your AI project collects form responses, uploads, account data, support messages, client materials, analytics, chat logs, prompts, files, or other information from people or businesses.

Use it before adding a new AI vendor, prompt, automation, file upload, logging feature, analytics tool, support flow, customer-facing workflow, or client workflow.

## What This Produces

You should finish with:

- a practical data map
- a list of data that should stay out of AI tools by default
- a vendor/tool data exposure record
- user notice and do-not-submit notes
- open questions for legal, privacy, security, client, platform, or vendor review

This worksheet does not decide legal conclusions. It helps you make data use visible before you launch.

## Plain Data Rule

Use this as a starting rule and edit it for the project:

> Do not put customer, client, buyer, employee, contractor, student, health, financial, legal, confidential, contract-restricted, credential, password, API key, or secret information into AI tools unless the specific tool and workflow have been reviewed for that type of data.

If the data came from someone else, assume it needs more care than your own notes.

## Safer Input Alternatives

Use these when AI help is useful but the raw data is not appropriate for the tool or workflow:

- replace names with placeholders
- summarize facts without personal details
- use fictional examples
- ask for structure, questions, checklists, or review prompts instead of giving private facts
- work from public information only
- use a reviewed business or enterprise AI account when sensitive data is truly needed
- get client approval before using AI on client data
- keep credentials, keys, payment details, and passwords out of prompts entirely

## Review Carefully If

- You send user data, client data, confidential files, sensitive categories, credentials, payment data, or information about children to a third-party AI vendor.
- Logs, analytics, support tools, session replay, or error reports may capture prompts, uploaded files, or outputs.
- Client contracts, platform rules, privacy promises, or professional duties may restrict AI use.
- Users are not clearly told what data should not be submitted.
- You are unsure whether users have been told AI is involved.

Short boundary: This worksheet is educational information, not legal advice. It helps you document data touching an AI workflow and spot questions to review. Review specific legal, privacy, contractual, security, platform, or regulatory obligations with qualified counsel or the appropriate advisor.
