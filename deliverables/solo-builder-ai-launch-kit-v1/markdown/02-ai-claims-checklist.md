# AI Claims Checklist

Purpose: Review what you say your AI can do before users, customers, clients, platforms, ad reviewers, or marketplace reviewers see the claim.

## When To Use It

Use this before publishing or updating a landing page, product page, marketplace listing, demo, ad, email sequence, social post, pitch deck, sales script, onboarding screen, FAQ, proposal, or client-facing deliverable.

Use it again when you change the AI feature, model, prompt, workflow, audience, pricing, target customer, or public positioning.

## What This Produces

You should finish with:

- a list of important claims
- notes on what evidence supports each claim
- a risk rating for each claim
- safer replacement wording for weak or overstated claims
- a publish decision for each claim

## Step 2: Check Claim Types

### AI Capability Claims

- [ ] Do you say the AI writes, summarizes, classifies, recommends, decides, detects, predicts, monitors, reviews, or automates something?
- [ ] Is the exact task narrow enough for the real workflow?
- [ ] Does the claim make clear whether the AI creates a draft, suggestion, score, answer, or final output?
- [ ] Does the claim avoid implying the AI can handle every edge case?

### Accuracy, Quality, And Reliability Claims

- [ ] Do you say the AI is accurate, precise, reliable, consistent, current, high-quality, error-free, or always available?
- [ ] Do you explain where the output can be incomplete, outdated, wrong, generic, or context-dependent?
- [ ] Do you have examples, tests, review notes, customer evidence, or other support for the exact claim?
- [ ] Does the claim avoid implying professional certainty where there is none?

### Automation And Human Review Claims

- [ ] Do you say the AI fully automates a task?
- [ ] Is a human still reviewing, editing, approving, sending, publishing, or making a decision?
- [ ] Does the copy say who is responsible for final review?
- [ ] Does the workflow avoid hiding automation where the user would expect a person?
- [ ] Does the product fail safely if the AI gives weak, incomplete, or unexpected output?

### Earnings, Savings, Productivity, Or Business Outcome Claims

- [ ] Do you say the AI saves money, saves time, increases revenue, gets clients, improves conversion, improves ranking, reduces churn, resolves support tickets, or creates a business result?
- [ ] Is the claim based on real evidence or only hope, examples, anecdotes, or competitor language?
- [ ] Does the wording avoid promising results that depend on the user's market, skill, budget, data, or execution?
- [ ] If you use a number, can you explain where it came from?

### Professional Advice Or High-Stakes Claims

- [ ] Could a user think the AI gives legal, medical, mental health, financial, tax, HR, safety, education, housing, credit, insurance, or other professional advice?
- [ ] Could the output affect access, eligibility, pricing, benefits, employment, services, or important opportunities?
- [ ] Does the page say what the tool is not for?
- [ ] Is there qualified human review where the context requires more than a template?

### Trust, Safety, Compliance, Or Approval Claims

- [ ] Do you use words like safe, compliant, certified, approved, guaranteed, risk-free, regulator-ready, enforcement-proof, human-level, expert-level, unbiased, secure, private, anonymous, or fully automated?
- [ ] Can you support the exact word in the exact context?
- [ ] If not, rewrite it into a narrower statement about what the tool helps the user document, draft, review, or prepare.

## Step 3: Rewrite Risky Claim Patterns

Use this table to turn broad promises into narrower, more useful wording.

| Risky claim pattern | Why it is risky | Safer direction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| This makes your AI compliant. | Implies a legal or regulatory result. | Helps you document key AI project facts and spot questions to review. |
| Protects you from liability. | Implies a legal protection. | Helps you keep a practical record of claims, data use, disclosures, incidents, and changes. |
| Legally safe AI launch. | Implies a legal conclusion. | A plain-English starting point for preparing your AI project before launch. |
| Guaranteed accurate output. | Implies certainty the AI may not provide. | Draft output that should be reviewed before use. |
| Fully automates customer support. | Hides whether a person reviews or sends replies. | Drafts suggested replies that a person can review before sending. |
| Human-level expert advice. | Implies expertise and reliability. | Provides general AI-generated suggestions that may need qualified review. |
| All you need for compliance. | Overstates the role of a template. | A lightweight documentation kit, not a substitute for legal or regulatory review. |
| Writes high-converting copy. | Promises a business outcome. | Drafts first-pass copy options you can review, edit, and test. |
| Works with any customer data. | Overbroad data claim. | Designed for [SPECIFIC DATA TYPES]; users should avoid [DATA TO AVOID]. |

## Step 5: Final Claim Check

- [ ] The claim describes what the AI actually does today.
- [ ] The claim is narrow enough for the real data, model, prompt, and workflow.
- [ ] The claim does not hide whether output is reviewed by a person.
- [ ] The claim does not promise accuracy, safety, compliance, professional advice, business results, or user outcomes unless that exact claim has appropriate support.
- [ ] The claim matches the disclosure, FAQ, product behavior, and customer support process.
- [ ] Any claim needing legal, privacy, platform, professional, or sector review is marked before publication.

## Review Carefully If

- The claim appears in paid ads, marketplace listings, pricing pages, investor materials, client contracts, or checkout pages.
- The claim involves compliance, safety, privacy, security, certification, approval, professional advice, earnings, accuracy, automation, or real-world outcomes.
- The AI output could affect a person's rights, money, health, job, education, housing, insurance, credit, access to services, or safety.

Short boundary: This worksheet is educational information, not legal advice. It helps you document and review AI claims before launch. Review specific legal, privacy, advertising, contractual, regulatory, or professional obligations with qualified counsel or the appropriate advisor.
