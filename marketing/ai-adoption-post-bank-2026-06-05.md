## Short Posts

### 1. The first AI governance file

Most new businesses do not need a huge AI governance program on day one.

They need one working file:

- What AI tools are we using?
- Who owns each tool?
- What data should stay out?
- Which outputs need human review?
- What changes need to be tracked?

That is the first step. Not a committee. Not a 90-page manual. A visible file people can actually use.

### 2. Your vendor has terms. Your team still needs rules.

A vendor trust center does not tell your team what they are allowed to paste into an AI tool.

It does not assign an internal owner.

It does not decide whether the workflow is low risk or needs review.

It does not track model, vendor, prompt, or workflow changes over time.

Vendor documentation matters. Internal rules still matter too.

### 3. ChatGPT policy starter

A basic ChatGPT policy for employees should answer five questions:

1. Which tools are approved?
2. What data is off limits?
3. When is human review required?
4. What uses are prohibited?
5. Who answers questions before someone tries a new tool?

Keep the first version short enough that people can actually follow it.

Link: https://airegready.com/chatgpt-policy-for-employees

### 5. First-pass risk review

A first-pass AI risk review is not about answering every legal question.

It is about surfacing the right facts:

- What does the tool do?
- What data goes in?
- Who is affected?
- Where does output go?
- Could it affect decisions about people?
- Who reviews the output?
- What could go wrong?

That is enough to decide: approve, approve with conditions, pause, or escalate.

A simple employee rule:

Low-risk AI use still needs basic rules.

## Longer Posts

### 11. Why AI governance feels bigger than it is

AI governance sounds like an enterprise phrase.

For a founder or small business owner, it can be much simpler.

Start with four records:

1. AI use inventory - what tools are being used, by whom, for what purpose.
2. Acceptable use rules - what is allowed, what data is prohibited, when human review is required.
3. Risk intake - a way to review new tools or higher-risk workflows before they spread.
4. Update tracker - a log for vendor, model, prompt, workflow, and policy changes.

That is not everything a larger company may eventually need.

But it is a real first step.

The danger is waiting until the business has scattered tools, unclear practices, and no memory of who approved what.

### 14. AI vendor review is the next obvious pain

Once a business writes down its AI tools, the next question arrives quickly:

Can we approve this vendor?

That question shows up when:

- a team wants a new AI SaaS tool

## Side-Gig Posts

### 16. Before you sell the AI thing

If you are using AI to make side money, create a first file before you sell, publish, advertise, or take clients.

It does not need to be complicated:

- tools used
- data that stays out
- claims checked
- disclosure decision
- human review
- platform/client terms checked
- open questions

The goal is not enterprise governance.

The goal is avoiding obvious mistakes while the side gig becomes real.

### 17. What not to paste into AI for client work

Freelancers using AI in client work need one plain rule:

Do not paste client data, confidential files, contracts, credentials, private messages, financial details, legal facts, health information, or anything covered by an NDA into AI tools unless the client, contract, tool, and workflow allow it.

AI can help with structure, outlines, examples, and editing.

Client data needs more care.

### 18. AI wrote the sales copy. You still own the claim.

AI can make a product page sound confident fast.

That is useful and dangerous.

Before posting or running ads, check every claim:

- Is it true?
- Can I prove it?
- Is it overstated?
- Does it sound like a guarantee?
- Does it imply legal, financial, medical, tax, employment, or other expert outcomes?
- Would a buyer reasonably expect disclosure that AI helped create it?

AI can draft the copy. You still own the claim.

### 19. Side-gig AI readiness

Most side-gig builders do not need a 90-page AI governance manual.

They need a short file that answers:

- What AI tools did I use?
- What did AI help create?
- What data did I avoid?
- What did I check before launch?
- What should I disclose?
- What claims did I soften or remove?
- What questions need a lawyer, accountant, client, platform, or vendor answer?

That is enough to make the work visible before it turns into a real business.

### 20. The risky side-gig buckets

AI side gigs deserve extra caution when they touch:

- legal, financial, medical, tax, employment, education, housing, or insurance advice
- sensitive customer or client data
- hiring, scoring, eligibility, pricing, or access decisions
- real voices, faces, names, brands, or likenesses
- client contracts, NDAs, or confidential files
- platform rules about AI-generated content

Those are not always impossible.

They are just not publish-first-think-later territory.
