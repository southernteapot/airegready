## 1. The First AI File Every New Business Should Create

Best use: blog article, LinkedIn post, newsletter section.

Core idea: before a new business writes a big AI policy, it should create one plain working file that makes AI use visible.

Draft:

Most new businesses do not need an AI governance program on day one.

They need one working file.

That file should answer the questions that actually matter when AI use is already happening:

- What AI tools are being used?
- Who owns each tool or workflow?
- What business task is each tool supporting?
- What data is allowed and not allowed?
- Which outputs need human review?
- Which uses are low risk, which need review, and which should be paused?
- What changed since the last review?

The first AI file is not meant to solve every legal, security, privacy, or HR question. It is meant to make the current state visible enough that better decisions can happen.

Without it, AI use spreads through personal accounts, free trials, vendor features, meeting tools, browser extensions, support systems, design tools, and code assistants before anyone can say what the business is actually using.

That is the avoidable mess.

Start with one file. Keep it short. Update it when the tool list changes.

## 2. Your AI Policy Should Start With What People Are Already Doing

Best use: blog article or practical LinkedIn post.

Core idea: a useful AI policy starts from observed workflows, not abstract rules.

Draft:

A lot of first AI policies fail because they are written as if the business is starting from zero.

Most businesses are not starting from zero.

People are already using ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, AI meeting notes, AI search, AI writing tools, customer-support AI, design tools, and AI features inside existing SaaS products.

So the first policy question is not:

What should our ideal AI program look like?

It is:

What are people already doing, and which parts need rules?

Start by writing down the real workflows:

- drafting customer emails
- summarizing meetings
- writing code
- generating images
- analyzing documents
- answering support tickets
- creating sales copy
- researching prospects
- editing resumes or applications
- using vendor tools with built-in AI features

Then add plain rules around data, review, approval, prohibited uses, and escalation.

A first AI policy should be grounded in the actual work. Otherwise it becomes a document people ignore while the real AI stack keeps growing around it.

## 3. The Hidden AI Stack: What To Inventory Before Writing Rules

Best use: blog article, search-support content, Gumroad update.

Core idea: the AI stack is usually larger than official purchases, so inventory should include embedded and informal AI use.

Draft:

Your AI stack is probably bigger than your software budget suggests.

It is not only the tools the business officially bought.

It can include:

- personal ChatGPT accounts
- Claude or Gemini workspaces
- Microsoft Copilot
- AI meeting transcription
- AI search tools
- CRM AI features
- email and document assistants
- AI browser extensions
- customer-support chat tools
- design and image generators
- code assistants
- recruiting or HR tools with AI scoring or summaries
- analytics tools with AI summaries
- vendor dashboards that quietly added AI features

That is why an AI use inventory is the practical first step.

The goal is not to make a perfect asset register. The goal is to answer a smaller set of questions:

- Where is AI being used?
- Who owns it?
- What data touches it?
- Is the tool approved, experimental, paused, or unknown?
- Does the output affect customers, employees, pricing, eligibility, or public claims?
- When should it be reviewed again?

Once the hidden AI stack is visible, policy writing gets easier. Without the inventory, the policy is mostly guessing.

## 4. Low-Risk AI Use Still Needs Rules

Best use: short article or social post.

Core idea: low risk should mean lighter process, not no process.

Draft:

Some AI use really is low risk.

Internal brainstorming. Formatting a draft. Summarizing a public article. Turning rough notes into a checklist. Generating placeholder copy that a person rewrites.

Those uses should not require enterprise-level approval.

But low risk does not mean no rules.

Even low-risk use needs basic boundaries:

- use approved tools when possible
- do not paste sensitive or restricted data
- do not treat AI output as automatically accurate
- review before sending externally
- disclose or escalate when the use affects important decisions
- log new recurring workflows so the business knows they exist

The practical goal is proportional review.

Low-risk AI should be easy to use safely. Higher-risk AI should be reviewed before it spreads. Prohibited uses should be plain enough that a contractor or new employee can understand them in five minutes.

That is much more useful than pretending all AI use belongs in the same bucket.

## 5. The AI Vendor Question Comes Sooner Than Founders Expect

Best use: article promoting future Vendor AI Review Packet, product #2 positioning.

Core idea: once a business inventories AI use, vendor approval becomes the obvious next problem.

Draft:

The first AI governance question is usually internal:

What tools are we using, and what rules do people need?

The second question comes fast:

Can we approve this AI vendor?

That question shows up when a team wants to use a new AI SaaS tool, a current vendor adds AI features, a consultant recommends a platform, customer data might enter the tool, or an AI feature starts affecting public output.

A useful vendor review does not need to start as a heavy procurement process.

It should collect the basic facts:

- what the tool does
- what data the tool receives
- whether customer, employee, student, health, financial, legal, or confidential data is involved
- whether inputs or outputs are used to train models
- what retention and deletion options exist
- what security and privacy documents the vendor provides
- what humans review before the output matters
- what contract terms, red flags, and exit issues need attention

The point is not to answer every legal or security question inside one template.

The point is to organize the facts so the business can decide whether to approve, approve with limits, pause, reject, or escalate.

## 6. The Five-Minute ChatGPT Rule For Employees

Best use: LinkedIn post, short guide, practical checklist.

Core idea: if an employee or contractor cannot understand the AI rule quickly, it will not guide behavior.

Draft:

A useful ChatGPT rule should be understandable in five minutes.

Start with this:

Use approved AI tools for approved work. Do not paste customer, employee, student, health, financial, legal, confidential, regulated, or contract-restricted data into an AI tool unless the tool and workflow have been approved for that data. Review AI output before using it with customers, clients, applicants, employees, investors, regulators, or the public. Ask before using AI for hiring, pricing, eligibility, legal, medical, financial, disciplinary, or other high-impact decisions.

That is not a complete policy.

But it gives people a real rule before the complete policy exists.

Then the business can add:

- approved tool list
- allowed use examples
- prohibited use examples
- human review requirements
- escalation path
- update owner
- review schedule

The first version should be boring, specific, and easy to follow.

## 7. Do Not Let A Vendor Trust Center Become Your AI Policy

Best use: social post, newsletter, vendor review article.

Core idea: vendor documents matter, but they do not replace internal rules and ownership.

Draft:

A vendor trust center can be useful.

It may explain security controls, privacy terms, subprocessors, data retention, training practices, certifications, support channels, and enterprise features.

But it does not answer the internal questions for your business:

- Who is allowed to use this tool?
- What data are they allowed to put into it?
- Which workflows are approved?
- Which outputs require human review?
- What uses are prohibited?
- Who owns the vendor relationship?
- What happens when the vendor changes a model, feature, term, or retention setting?

Vendor documentation is evidence. It is not your policy.

The business still needs an internal decision record: approved, approved with limits, paused, rejected, or escalated.

This is especially important for AI features embedded in tools people already use. A vendor may add AI functionality quietly, but internal use still needs a clear owner and rule.

## 8. The First AI Change Log

Best use: blog article or follow-up post after inventory/policy pieces.

Core idea: AI governance breaks when nobody tracks changes to tools, models, prompts, workflows, vendors, or rules.

Draft:

AI use changes quickly.

The tool changes. The model changes. The prompt changes. The workflow changes. A vendor adds a feature. Someone connects a new data source. A contractor starts using a different assistant. A policy exception becomes normal practice.

If nobody tracks those changes, the original AI review gets stale fast.

A lightweight AI change log should capture:

- date
- tool or workflow
- owner
- what changed
- why it changed
- data or output affected
- whether risk tier changed
- reviewer or approver
- next review date

This does not need to be complicated.

The point is memory.

When a customer, investor, partner, employee, or reviewer asks how AI use is controlled, the business should be able to show that changes are noticed and recorded.

## 9. What To Ask Before AI Touches Customer Data

Best use: practical article, high-intent founder/operator content.

Core idea: customer data should trigger a short review before AI use spreads.

Draft:

Before an AI tool touches customer data, pause long enough to answer a few practical questions.

Start here:

- What customer data will be used?
- Is the data confidential, sensitive, regulated, or contract-restricted?
- Was the customer told this kind of use could happen?
- Does the vendor use inputs or outputs for model training?
- How long is the data retained?
- Can the business delete it?
- Who can access the data inside the vendor system?
- Is the output customer-facing?
- Does a human review the output before it matters?
- What happens if the output is wrong?

This is not a substitute for legal, privacy, security, or contract review when those issues matter.

It is the first-pass screen that helps a founder or operator decide whether the workflow is simple, needs limits, should be paused, or should be escalated.

## 10. A Practical AI Readiness Stack For Solo Founders

Best use: newsletter, blog article, product comparison post.

Core idea: solo founders need a small set of records, not an enterprise program.

Draft:

Solo founders using AI need a practical readiness stack.

Not a large governance office. Not an enterprise manual. Not a pile of unread policies.

The first stack can be simple:

1. AI use inventory - the tools and workflows already in use.
2. Approved use rules - what is allowed, blocked, and review-required.
3. Risk intake - a quick screen for new tools and higher-risk workflows.
4. Vendor notes - terms, privacy, security, training, retention, and deletion facts.
5. Change log - updates to tools, models, prompts, workflows, vendors, and rules.
6. Open questions list - items to ask counsel, privacy, security, HR, or procurement when the business reaches that point.

That stack gives the founder a starting file.

It will not answer every regulatory question. It will not replace qualified review for specific obligations. But it can stop AI use from becoming invisible.

## 12. The AI Workflow That Should Not Be Approved In A Slack Thread

Best use: punchier LinkedIn post, short article, vendor/risk angle.

Core idea: higher-impact AI workflows need a decision record, not informal chat approval.

Draft:

Some AI workflows should not be approved in a Slack thread.

Examples:

- AI used in hiring screens
- AI used for pricing or eligibility
- AI used with sensitive customer data
- AI used in legal, financial, medical, employment, housing, insurance, or education contexts
- AI output sent directly to customers
- AI connected to internal systems
- AI features from a vendor that changed terms, retention, or training practices

Those workflows may or may not be appropriate.

But they deserve more than "looks fine" in a chat message.

At minimum, keep a decision record:

- what the workflow does
- what data it uses
- who is affected
- what could go wrong
- who reviews the output
- what limits apply
- who approved it
- when it should be reviewed again

That is the difference between informal adoption and a visible process.

## 13. Before You Sell The AI Thing

Best use: first side-gig landing page, Gumroad update, social post, ad concept.

Core idea: people using AI to make side income need a first file before they sell, publish, advertise, or take clients.

Draft:

If you are using AI to make a little money on the side, the first useful question is not whether you need an enterprise AI governance program.

You probably do not.

The useful question is:

What should I write down before I sell this?

Start with a simple file:

- what AI tools you used

- what customer or client data stayed out

- what you decided to disclose
- what a human reviewed

That file will not guarantee anything.

But it makes the work visible. It gives you a record. It helps you catch obvious mistakes before the side gig becomes public.

## 14. AI Wrote The Sales Copy. You Still Own The Claim.

Best use: side-gig ad angle, article, product-page support content.

Core idea: AI can draft confident copy, but the seller still owns the claims.

Draft:

AI can write a product page in thirty seconds.

That does not mean the claims are safe.

Before publishing a side-gig product page or running ads, check the important claims:

- Is this true?
- Can I prove it?
- Does it imply a guarantee?
- Does it imply expert review I did not get?
- Does it sound like legal, financial, medical, tax, employment, education, housing, insurance, or other professional advice?
- Does it promise a result the product cannot control?
- Would a buyer care that AI helped create it?

AI can make weak claims sound polished. That is the risk.

The seller still owns the final wording.

## 15. What Not To Paste Into AI For Client Work

Best use: freelancer/consultant post or article.

Core idea: client data is the first practical risk for AI-assisted freelance work.

Draft:

Freelancers using AI in client work need one plain rule:

Do not paste client data into AI tools unless the client, contract, tool, and workflow allow it.

That includes:

- confidential files
- contracts
- private messages
- customer lists
- financial details
- legal facts
- health information
- unreleased strategy
- proprietary code
- credentials, passwords, API keys, or secrets
- anything covered by an NDA

AI can still help.

Ask for structure. Use placeholders. Summarize without private details. Create fictional examples. Get client permission when the workflow needs real client data.

The goal is not to avoid AI. The goal is to avoid putting the wrong data into the wrong tool.
