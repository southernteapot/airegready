> **How to use this policy.** Synthetic media — images, audio, and video generated or materially altered by AI — creates specific legal, reputational, and ethical risks. This policy establishes rules for creating, using, and labeling synthetic media at [COMPANY NAME].

---

## 1. Scope

Applies to any synthetic media:

- Created by [COMPANY NAME] using AI tools (image, voice, video generators)
- Distributed by [COMPANY NAME] (in marketing, product, internal use, or public communications)
- Hosted by [COMPANY NAME] on platforms where users can post

## 2. Definitions

- **Synthetic media** — audio, image, or video that is wholly or materially generated or modified by an AI system
- **Deepfake** — synthetic media depicting a real identifiable person saying or doing things the person did not say or do
- **Manifest disclosure** — visible label indicating AI generation
- **Latent disclosure** — machine-readable provenance (watermark, metadata, C2PA credential)

## 3. Principles

1. **Consent** — no real person in synthetic media without written, specific consent
2. **Disclosure** — label AI-generated content where authorship matters or law requires
3. **Truthfulness** — don't use synthetic media to deceive
4. **Respect for likeness** — honor right-of-publicity and state likeness laws
5. **Provenance** — apply machine-readable provenance where feasible

## 4. Permitted uses

With proper clearance:

- AI-generated stock imagery not depicting identifiable real people
- Generic illustrations and backgrounds
- Product visualizations where no real person is used
- Training and educational material with clear AI authorship
- Concept art and internal drafts
- Accessible content (e.g., AI-generated descriptions for alt text)

## 5. Prohibited uses

Regardless of other provisions:

- Depicting any identifiable real person without that person's written, specific consent
- Depicting any deceased person in a commercial context without the estate's written consent (where applicable)
- Voice cloning of real persons without consent (Tennessee ELVIS Act; state right of publicity)
- Deepfakes representing actions, words, or identities falsely attributed to real persons
- Non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) — prohibited absolutely
- CSAM or material that simulates CSAM — prohibited absolutely
- Content intended to manipulate elections or undermine democratic processes
- Content intended to deceive consumers about the identity or endorsement of real persons
- Content that violates trademark, copyright, or other IP

## 6. Consent requirements for real persons

When a real person is depicted:

- **Written consent** specific to the synthetic-media use
- Consent covers the scope (purpose, format, medium, duration)
- Consent identifies any commercial uses
- Consent identifies the tools / vendors involved
- Consent disclosed any restrictions or limits
- Revocation process available
- Records retained

Template consent form maintained in the legal-form library.

## 7. Use of employees, customers, and public figures

- **Employees**: consent must be freely given; scope limited; do not tie to continued employment; provide clear withdrawal path
- **Customers**: consent per [COMPANY NAME]'s model-release process; do not misrepresent
- **Public figures**: additional caution; political contexts especially restricted; check election-period rules (California AB 2655, similar)
- **Deceased**: estate consent; post-mortem right of publicity varies by state

## 8. Manifest disclosure requirements

Label synthetic media as AI-generated in the following contexts:

- Public-facing marketing where reasonable viewers would expect a real photo
- Editorial content where authorship matters
- News or informational content
- Chatbot avatars and voice agents
- Synthetic voice in automated calls
- Product UX where users might assume human creation

Recommended label formats:

- Image captions: "AI-generated image" or "Illustration created with AI"
- Video: visible label in frame or in description
- Audio: spoken disclosure at the start ("This message uses an AI voice")

## 9. Latent disclosure / provenance

Where technically feasible:

- **C2PA** content credentials on images and video
- **Metadata** tags indicating AI generation
- **Watermarks** that survive common transformations
- **Hash-based provenance** with our content ledger

Required by:

- California SB 942 (for providers above threshold)
- EU AI Act Art 50(4) for synthetic content
- Emerging industry standards and customer expectations

## 10. Platform / UGC

For platforms that host user-generated synthetic media:

- Terms prohibit unauthorized real-person content
- Detection tools for deepfakes, NCII, CSAM
- Takedown process with SLAs
- Repeat-infringer policy
- Transparency reporting (where appropriate)

## 11. Political and election contexts

Election-adjacent synthetic media has specific rules in many jurisdictions:

- California AB 2655 and related — deceptive election media
- State-level election deepfake laws (growing list)
- FCC guidance on AI voice calls in political contexts
- Platform policies during election windows

Policy:

- No synthetic media of candidates or election officials without consent
- No synthetic media intended to influence voting through deception
- Enhanced review during election windows
- Cooperate with platform requirements

## 12. Sector-specific cautions

### News and journalism

- Do not publish synthetic media as if it were a real photograph of events
- Labeling must be prominent and survive social re-shares
- Editorial judgment applies

### Entertainment and creative

- AI-generated performers require clearance if resembling real persons
- Union agreements may restrict certain uses (SAG-AFTRA, WGA)
- Posthumous performances require estate involvement

### Advertising

- Endorsements must be truthful (FTC rules apply to synthetic "endorsers")
- Claims substantiated
- No misrepresentation of the product or its origin

### Healthcare

- No synthetic medical imagery presented as real without disclosure
- Synthetic-patient examples for education must be clearly labeled

### Dating / social

- No synthetic profile photos of fake people posing as real users
- Platforms required to disclose AI-generated profiles per emerging law

## 13. Production pipeline

Every synthetic media asset in our pipeline carries:

- Prompt / source description
- Model / vendor used
- Created date and user
- Subject classification (real person? identifiable? public figure?)
- Use-case classification
- Clearance / consent records
- Disclosure / provenance applied
- Intended distribution channels

## 14. Review and approval

- **Internal drafts**: lightweight
- **Customer-facing non-person content**: brand review
- **Content depicting people**: legal review
- **Political / elections / sensitive**: legal + executive review

## 15. Training

- Brand and marketing trained on policy
- Creative and content staff trained on consent workflows
- Customer support trained on takedown intake
- Legal trained on cross-state synthetic-media rules

## 16. Incident handling

- Complaints from depicted individuals — urgent response
- Platform takedown notices — rapid turnaround
- Discovered unauthorized synthetic media on our platforms — takedown + account action
- Regulatory inquiries — coordinated Legal response

## 17. Common traps

- Believing "fair use" applies to synthetic likeness
- Using influencer / celebrity faces in ads without consent
- Under-labeling AI-generated marketing imagery
- Missing state deepfake rules
- Production pipelines that don't track consent
- Voice agents without disclosure (bot disclosure rules)
