# Tennessee ELVIS Act — Readiness Kit Overview

**Regulation:** Ensuring Likeness Voice and Image Security Act (ELVIS Act), Tenn. Code Ann. § 47-25-1101 et seq. (amending and renaming the Tennessee Personal Rights Protection Act)
**Effective:** July 1, 2024
**Enforcement:** Individuals and record-label assignees (private right of action); criminal penalties for certain violations

> **How to use this kit.** For anyone generating, distributing, or enabling synthetic voice or likeness content that could reach Tennessee consumers, anyone building voice-cloning technology, and any company whose products could be used to clone a real person's voice without authorization. Pair with the Synthetic Media Policy and the Customer AI Disclosure Templates.

## 1. Why the ELVIS Act matters

Tennessee's ELVIS Act expanded the state's existing right-of-publicity protections to explicitly cover an individual's **voice**, in addition to name, photograph, and likeness. The Act was motivated by the rise of generative AI voice cloning and particularly the concerns of Nashville's music industry.

ELVIS Act consequences include civil damages, injunctive relief, and criminal liability for certain uses. The private right of action has driven early enforcement.

## 2. What's protected

- **Voice** — a sound in a medium that is readily identifiable and attributable to a particular individual, regardless of whether the sound contains the actual voice or a simulation of the voice
- **Name**
- **Photograph**
- **Likeness** (visual appearance)

The protections apply to living individuals and, under Tennessee law, may continue after death for descendibility periods.

## 3. Prohibited activities

Using protected attributes (name, voice, photograph, likeness) of another person without authorization in:

- Advertising
- Solicitation of purchases
- Endorsements
- Commercial products or services
- Distribution of sound recordings or audiovisual works that use a voice or likeness

Specifically in response to AI, ELVIS adds civil liability for:

- **Publishing, performing, distributing, transmitting, or otherwise making available to the public** a sound recording or audiovisual work where the voice or likeness has been used without authorization
- **Distributing, transmitting, or making available an algorithm, software, tool, or other technology** with knowledge that its primary purpose or function is the production of unauthorized voice or likeness use

## 4. Who is at risk

- **Generative AI providers** whose tools can produce synthetic voices / likenesses of real persons
- **Content platforms** hosting user-generated voice / likeness content
- **Marketers and advertisers** using AI-generated voices of real people without consent
- **Podcast / audiobook creators** using AI voices modeled on real persons
- **Influencer economy participants** who generate fake endorsements
- **Political campaigns** using synthetic media of opponents
- **Entertainment industry** — deepfakes, de-aged actors, posthumous performances

## 5. Authorization and consent

- Written consent from the individual or their authorized representative
- Consent must cover the specific use contemplated
- Consent for one use does not extend to others
- Posthumous uses require consent from the estate / heirs; Tennessee's post-mortem right of publicity applies

## 6. Exceptions

The Act contains limited exceptions, including:

- Use in news, public affairs, sports broadcasts (incidental or historical contexts)
- Use in commentary, criticism, scholarship, satire, parody
- Fleeting / de minimis use in certain contexts
- Use by owners or licensees of copyrighted works for promotional purposes related to the work

Exceptions are narrow and context-dependent. Do not rely on a general "fair use" mindset — Tennessee's standard is specific.

## 7. Technology-provider liability

The "algorithm, software, tool, or other technology" provision is broad. A generative AI provider whose tool is primarily used to clone real voices without authorization faces potential liability. Structural defenses:

- Restricting the tool to voices with verified consent (e.g., uploaded voice samples with authorization)
- Content moderation and takedown processes
- Purpose limitations in Terms of Service
- Detection of celebrity voice imitations and refusal

## 8. Practical readiness path

1. **Inventory** your voice / likeness capabilities
2. **Restrict** tools from producing voices of identifiable real persons without verified consent
3. **Terms** that prohibit users from creating unauthorized real-person voice / likeness content
4. **Detection** — monitor for misuse; refuse / remove
5. **Consent workflow** — where real persons are the subject, capture written, specific consent
6. **Posthumous policy** — extra caution with deceased persons' estates
7. **Platform posture** — if you host UGC, implement takedown and repeat-infringer processes
8. **Marketing discipline** — no real-person voices in ads without consent

## 9. Related state laws

Tennessee is not alone. Monitor:

- **California** SB 1001 / AB 2602 / AB 1836 / SB 926 — various rules on synthetic media and digital replicas
- **New York** right of publicity expansion
- **Illinois** HB 4875 (the Digital Replica Act)
- **Louisiana, Washington, others** — growing patchwork
- **Federal** — NO FAKES Act proposals (pending)
- **EU AI Act Art 50** — deepfake disclosure obligations

## 10. Common traps

- Assuming "fair use" applies because the output is transformative
- Using a celebrity's voice in a training dataset without consent
- Hosting user-generated deepfakes without takedown
- Marketing "voice AI" capabilities without consent controls
- Commercial use of a deceased person's voice without estate approval
- Ignoring cross-state exposure — Tennessee plaintiffs can pursue out-of-state defendants
