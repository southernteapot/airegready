> **How to use this guide.** You manage people who use AI. This guide covers how to set expectations, handle common situations, and raise concerns when things go sideways. Short, practical, not a rule book.

---

## Your five responsibilities

1. **Set expectations** — your team follows the AI Acceptable Use Policy; you make sure they know it
2. **Enable good use** — approved tools, training, and scenarios your team can actually practice
3. **Watch for issues** — shadow AI, misuse, over-reliance, error patterns
4. **Handle incidents well** — tell the right people, don't punish good-faith reports
5. **Report up** — share concerns with leadership and AI Governance

---

## Week 1 for a new direct report

- Confirm they've completed the AI onboarding packet
- Confirm they've signed the AUP acknowledgment
- Walk through the approved tools for their role
- Share your team's specific AI norms (what you use, how, for what)
- Introduce the [#ai-questions] channel

## In 1:1s

- Ask occasionally: "How are you using AI lately?" — makes AI use visible without making it weird
- If someone mentions a new tool: ask whether it's on the approved list; if not, raise it with AI Governance

## Setting expectations on AI-assisted work

Be explicit about your expectations:

- **Review obligations**: if you write a memo with AI, you still own it
- **Citation / attribution**: use quality sources even when AI summarizes
- **Honesty**: don't pretend AI-drafted content is your own in contexts where authorship matters (e.g., a performance review of a direct report; a condolence to a coworker)
- **Quality bar**: AI-assisted doesn't mean "ship it"

## Common situations and how to handle them

### 1. A direct report tells you they pasted customer data into ChatGPT

Calm and fast:

1. Thank them for telling you
2. Confirm what was pasted and when
3. Escalate to [AI GOVERNANCE LEAD] or [SECURITY LEAD] immediately
4. Help the employee document what happened
5. Don't punish the good-faith report

The incident response team will handle the rest. Your job is to support.

### 2. Your team is quietly using a tool that's not approved

Shadow AI is common and usually well-intentioned. Approach:

1. Don't make an example of anyone
2. Say clearly: "I appreciate you found something that works. We need to get it on the approved list or find an alternative. Here's who to talk to."
3. Help them submit the tool for approval
4. Until approved, use the alternative path
5. Don't keep it a secret from AI Governance

### 3. A report's AI-assisted output has a big error that went public

1. Acknowledge — everyone has been there
2. Get the correction out fast (coordinate with the relevant function)
3. Root cause: was it reasonable to rely on AI for this? Was the review appropriate?
4. Individual consequences only if there was a real disregard of policy
5. Feed learnings into team norms

### 4. A report is over-relying on AI

Signs: outputs feel generic; team member can't explain their own work; mistakes becoming patterns.

Approach:

1. Direct feedback: "I want to see more of your own thinking in this area."
2. Specific scenarios where AI should be a starter, not the finisher
3. Training resources
4. Follow up

### 5. A candidate or employee complains about AI in a hiring or performance context

Take it seriously:

1. Listen fully
2. Don't dismiss — "the AI did it" is not a defense
3. Route to HR and AI Governance per the complaint handling procedure
4. Document the conversation

### 6. Your team is asked to do something with AI that feels wrong

If a request violates the AI policy, Code of Ethics, or applicable law:

1. Push back immediately with the requester
2. Flag to your own manager
3. Escalate to AI Governance Lead if unresolved
4. Whistleblower protections apply — use them

### 7. Your team wants to build something that sounds like Tier 3 (high-risk)

Great — but it needs the full governance path:

1. Route to the AI Review Committee
2. Full Impact Assessment before deployment
3. Bias / safety testing
4. Timeline and resources that reflect the additional rigor
5. Don't let "we need to ship fast" skip steps

## Performance management with AI

- AI can help draft, summarize, and structure. You still own the content of every review.
- Don't use AI to decide ratings. Use it to organize your thoughts.
- Don't paste sensitive performance notes into unapproved tools.
- Disclose AI assistance where appropriate (e.g., if a report asks).

## Hiring with AI

If your team hires:

- You are responsible for following the hiring workflow
- Candidate notice must be given for AEDT use
- 10-business-day lead time (NYC)
- Accommodation and alternative-selection requests are honored without discussion
- Adverse-action notices go out for AI-material rejections
- See the HR AI card

## Training and refresh

- Confirm annual AI refresher completion for every report
- Schedule team-level AI discussion at least yearly ("what's working, what's not")
- Contribute feedback to AI Governance on what's missing from training

## Escalation

Raise to AI Governance Lead when:

- You're unsure whether a use is OK
- You've seen a pattern of issues
- A report has raised a concern that hasn't been addressed
- You're seeing external signals (customer complaints, press, regulatory)

Raise to HR when:

- AI is affecting someone's employment / career
- Someone feels retaliated against for raising concerns
- An accommodation request hasn't been honored

Raise to Legal when:

- A claim is made about our AI (discrimination, breach of contract, privacy)
- A regulator has contacted the team

## What not to do as a manager

- **Don't set targets that implicitly require unapproved AI use** ("double our output this quarter")
- **Don't retaliate** against reporters
- **Don't single out individuals** for shadow AI in public
- **Don't paste employee records** into public AI tools to "get insights"
- **Don't make termination decisions** from AI recommendations alone
- **Don't claim AI abilities** in customer interactions that the product doesn't have

## Fitting AI into your team's work

Some practical patterns that work:

- **Drafting**: AI for first drafts, human for final
- **Summarization**: AI for big docs, human verifies key decisions
- **Research**: AI to scope, human to dig in
- **Code**: AI for boilerplate, human for logic and security
- **Meeting notes**: AI transcribes, human action items
- **Brainstorming**: AI for range, human for judgment

## A short word on well-being

AI changes the shape of work. Some people love it; others find it disorienting. Watch for:

- People who feel their work is being devalued
- People who feel they're losing skills
- People who feel surveilled
- People who feel overwhelmed by "now you should use AI"

Have honest conversations. Escalate to HR and leadership if patterns suggest larger issues.
