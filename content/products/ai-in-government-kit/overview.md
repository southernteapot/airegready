# AI in Government & Public Sector — Compliance Kit Overview

**Regulatory landscape:**

- **Federal** — OMB memos (M-24-10 governance, M-24-18 AI acquisition), NIST AI RMF as federal baseline, FISMA / FedRAMP for systems, Section 508 accessibility, Federal Records Act
- **State executive orders / laws** — many states have enacted or proposed AI governance for state agencies (NY, CA, TX TRAIGA, UT, etc.)
- **Constitutional law** — due process, equal protection, First Amendment for AI-assisted decisions
- **Public records / FOIA / state sunshine laws** — document production for AI records
- **Procurement rules** — FAR, state procurement codes, minority contracting
- **EU AI Act** — public authority deployers have FRIA and registration obligations
- **Anti-discrimination law** — Title VI, Title VII, Title IX, ADA, Rehab Act Section 504
- **Social services** — program-specific rules (HUD, SNAP, Medicaid, Unemployment)
- **Law enforcement / criminal justice** — specific constitutional and statutory limits

> **How to use this kit.** For federal, state, and local government agencies using AI, and for vendors serving public-sector clients. Pair with AI Governance Framework, Impact Assessment, and appropriate sector kit (Education, Healthcare, etc.).

---

## 1. Why government AI is different

- **Constitutional limits** apply to government action in ways they don't to private
- **Public accountability** — FOIA, sunshine laws, oversight bodies, media
- **Procurement rigor** — fair competition, small-business set-asides, buy-American, security
- **Equity obligations** — heightened for programs serving vulnerable populations
- **Direct harm potential** — benefits decisions, licensing, criminal justice can alter lives

## 2. OMB M-24-10 / M-24-18 essentials (federal)

- Agencies must designate a Chief AI Officer
- Maintain an AI Use Case Inventory (public-facing)
- Implement specific risk-management practices for rights-impacting and safety-impacting AI
- Use NIST AI RMF as the baseline framework
- Conduct impact assessments before deploying new rights-impacting AI
- Honor opt-out and human alternatives where appropriate
- Public notice and feedback mechanisms
- AI acquisition standards

State governments often track federal expectations with state-specific variations.

## 3. Rights-impacting vs. safety-impacting

- **Rights-impacting** — AI affecting civil rights, civil liberties, privacy, equal opportunity, access to education / employment / housing / credit / government benefits
- **Safety-impacting** — AI affecting human lives, physical safety, critical infrastructure, national security

Both categories trigger enhanced obligations under federal (and increasingly state) rules.

## 4. AI Use Case Inventory

Federal agencies are required to maintain a public inventory of AI uses with specified fields (purpose, stage, sensitivity, etc.). Vendors serving federal agencies should be prepared to support public disclosures about the systems they provide.

## 5. Constitutional concerns

- **Due process** — when AI affects benefits, licenses, employment, or liberty, due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard
- **Equal protection** — AI that disparately impacts protected classes requires justification
- **First Amendment** — AI moderation and surveillance in government contexts
- **Fourth Amendment** — AI-driven surveillance, predictive policing

## 6. EU AI Act — public bodies

EU member state agencies deploying high-risk AI must:

- Conduct a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (Art 27)
- Register the system in the EU database
- Honor transparency obligations
- Comply with general deployer duties

## 7. Social services

AI used in benefits eligibility, fraud detection for benefits, child welfare, and unemployment has been a source of major errors — wrongful denials, family separations, fraud allegations. Specific obligations:

- Human reviewer in consequential decisions
- Clear adverse-action explanations
- Right to appeal
- Transparent algorithms in litigation where required by court
- Compliance with program-specific federal rules (HUD, SNAP, Medicaid, UI)

## 8. Criminal justice

- Pretrial risk assessment AI
- Recidivism AI
- Facial recognition by law enforcement
- Evidence produced by AI

Each carries specific constitutional and statutory limits, variable by jurisdiction. Many have been banned or restricted (e.g., facial recognition by police in several cities/states).

## 9. Procurement

- Fair competition among vendors
- Minority / small business set-asides
- Requirements for vendor AI transparency
- Contract clauses for FRIA cooperation, bias testing, data rights
- Section 508 accessibility for federal procurements

## 10. Public records

- AI models, training data, prompts, outputs used in government decision-making may be subject to FOIA / state open-records laws
- Vendor confidentiality claims tested against open-records law
- Records retention for AI artifacts

## 11. Practical readiness path

1. **Inventory** all AI uses across the agency
2. **Classify** rights-impacting / safety-impacting / other
3. **Chief AI Officer** designated with budget and authority
4. **Risk management program** aligned to NIST RMF
5. **Impact assessments** for high-risk uses
6. **Public notice** and public inventory
7. **Human alternatives** for affected persons
8. **Procurement clauses** updated for AI
9. **FOIA / records** preparation
10. **Vendor oversight**

## 12. Common traps

- **Vendor claims of "our AI is fair"** accepted without agency-side testing
- **No FRIA / impact assessment** before deployment
- **No human reviewer** in consequential decisions
- **Inadequate notice** to affected persons
- **Unauditable black-box AI** in evidence-sensitive contexts
- **Missing records** when FOIA arrives
