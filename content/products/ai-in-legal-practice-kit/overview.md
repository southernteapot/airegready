# AI in Legal Practice — Compliance Kit Overview

**Audience:** law firms, in-house legal departments, legal tech vendors serving law firms
**Regulatory landscape:**

- **ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct** — competence (1.1), communication (1.4), confidentiality (1.6), billing (1.5), supervision (5.1, 5.3), candor to tribunal (3.3), unauthorized practice of law (5.5)
- **ABA Formal Opinion 512** (2024) — Generative AI and the ABA Model Rules
- **State bar ethics opinions** — NYC, CA, DC, FL, TX, WA, and many others have issued AI ethics opinions
- **Court-specific rules** — many federal and state courts now require disclosure of AI-assisted filings; some prohibit certain uses
- **Attorney-client privilege and work product doctrine** — as applied to AI tool usage
- **Law firm data security** — ABA Formal Opinions 477R (2017) and others; state data security laws
- **EU AI Act** — legal services is a consequential-decision / Annex III high-risk category for certain uses
- **Colorado AI Act** — legal services is a consequential-decision category

> **How to use this kit.** For U.S. law firms (any size), in-house legal departments, and vendors building tools for lawyers. Read the overview; work the checklist. Pair with the AI Governance Framework, the AI Acceptable Use Policy (lawyer-adapted), and the AI Vendor Risk Assessment.

## 1. Why legal practice AI is different

Lawyers hold a professional license. Missteps in AI use can:

- Violate ethics rules (malpractice risk)
- Waive client privilege or work product
- Draw sanctions from a court
- Result in discipline by the state bar
- Produce public embarrassment in a profession built on credibility

Several widely publicized incidents have shown what can go wrong: AI-generated fake cases cited in briefs, confidential client information uploaded to public tools, and biased outcomes in legal research.

## 2. ABA Model Rules — mapping

### Competence (Rule 1.1)

Duty of competence requires lawyers to keep abreast of technology. ABA Formal Opinion 512 confirms this includes understanding the benefits and risks of generative AI. In practice:

- Lawyers using AI must understand what it does, its limits, and its risks — at a level sufficient to supervise its output
- Firms should provide training

### Confidentiality (Rule 1.6)

- Lawyers must not expose client confidential information to unapproved AI tools
- Using AI that trains on inputs, without consent, can breach confidentiality
- Certain AI uses require client informed consent

### Communication (Rule 1.4)

- Clients are entitled to be informed about material matters, which may include material AI use in their representation
- Engagement letter language should cover AI

### Supervision (Rules 5.1, 5.3)

- Partners supervising lawyers using AI have duties to ensure compliance
- Non-lawyer staff using AI must be trained and supervised

### Candor to tribunal (Rule 3.3)

- Submitting AI-generated cases or facts without verification is sanctionable
- Lawyers must check citations and facts before filing

### Fees (Rule 1.5)

- Fees must be reasonable. AI that dramatically reduces hours may impact billing ethics
- Rebilling AI tool costs to clients requires disclosure

### Unauthorized practice of law (Rule 5.5)

- AI tools offered directly to consumers risk UPL
- Law firms deploying consumer-facing AI need careful scope

## 3. ABA Formal Opinion 512

Key points (paraphrased):

- GenAI is a resource, not a substitute for lawyer judgment
- Competence extends to understanding GenAI benefits and risks
- Confidentiality: lawyers must protect client information when using GenAI; tool selection matters
- Communication: informed consent may be required for certain uses; client should be aware of AI's role
- Fees: billing for time, efficiency gains, and tool costs must be reasonable and transparent
- Supervision: lawyer is responsible for AI outputs used in practice

## 4. State bar opinions — common themes

- Consent for AI processing of client confidential info
- Verification of AI-generated citations, case law, regulations
- Clear fee disclosures
- Document AI tool vetting
- Avoid AI tools that retain client data for training

## 5. Court-specific AI rules

Many federal district courts and a growing number of state courts have issued standing orders or local rules requiring:

- Disclosure of AI use in a filing
- Certification that cites and quotes have been verified
- Disclosure of AI-assisted drafts of expert reports or exhibits

Always check the applicable court's current standing orders before filing.

## 6. Privilege and work product

- Sending client confidential information to a third-party AI vendor may waive privilege
- Careful vendor selection (enterprise vs. consumer tools; contracts with no-training clauses)
- Work-product doctrine protections depend on reasonable precautions

## 7. Data security (law-firm specific)

- ABA Formal Opinion 477R expects reasonable efforts to protect client information
- Data security incidents involving client data trigger notice obligations to clients
- State data breach laws apply to law firms

## 8. Colorado AI Act — legal services

Legal services is a consequential-decision category. This can implicate:

- Legal tech vendors that provide automated advice to consumers
- Law firms delivering quasi-automated intake and advice

Impact assessments, consumer notice, adverse-action explanations, and appeal paths may be required.

## 9. EU AI Act — legal / judicial

Annex III includes:

- AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of judicial authorities or by alternative dispute resolution bodies to assist judicial authorities in researching and interpreting facts and the law and in applying the law to a concrete set of facts
- AI systems used to influence elections or voter behavior

Law firms as deployers of such systems have high-risk obligations.

## 10. Practical readiness path

1. **AI System Inventory** for every AI tool the firm uses (research, drafting, discovery, contract analytics, translation, practice management, marketing)
2. **Firm AI policy** covering confidentiality, tool approval, citation verification, supervision, and disclosure
3. **Engagement letter updates** to address AI use
4. **Vendor vetting** — enterprise tools, no training on firm/client data, DPAs
5. **Training program** for lawyers and staff, with CLE credit where available
6. **Citation verification protocol** — mandatory for any AI-assisted brief or memo
7. **Court rules tracking** — firm-wide calendar of AI disclosure requirements in relevant courts
8. **Client consent forms** for AI uses that warrant it
9. **Incident handling** — what to do when AI output is wrong, leaked, or challenged
10. **Billing guidance** — reasonable fees in the AI era

## 11. Specific scenarios and guidance

**"Can I draft a brief with AI?"**
Yes, with review. Verify every citation and factual claim. Check the court's standing orders. Apply the client-confidentiality rule to any facts you paste into the tool.

**"Can I put a client's documents into ChatGPT to summarize?"**
Only in an enterprise tool with no training on your data, covered by a vendor agreement, and where client consent (explicit or via engagement letter) covers the use.

**"Can AI handle client intake?"**
Within limits. Unauthorized practice of law risk is real. Keep the AI to information collection, not legal advice. Human lawyer reviews before any advice is given.

**"Can I charge for the time I saved with AI?"**
Reasonableness standard applies. Discuss with clients and consider fee arrangements (value-based fees, efficiency discounts, or clear AI-assisted tiers).

**"The AI hallucinated a case."**
Do not submit. If discovered post-submission, notify the court and opposing counsel promptly. Mitigate, correct, and cooperate.

## 12. Common traps

- **Using consumer AI tools with client data**
- **Forgetting to verify citations**
- **Missing court disclosure rules**
- **Not updating engagement letters**
- **Inadequate supervision of junior lawyers / paralegals using AI**
- **Overcharging for AI-assisted work**
- **Providing quasi-automated legal advice outside UPL guardrails**
