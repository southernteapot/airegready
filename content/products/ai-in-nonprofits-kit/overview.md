# AI in Nonprofits — Compliance Kit Overview

**Audience:** 501(c)(3) and other nonprofit organizations of all sizes; foundations; associations; faith-based organizations; advocacy groups; community-based organizations.

**Regulatory landscape (summary):**

- **IRS rules** on tax-exempt activities — AI shouldn't create UBTI problems or jeopardize exempt status
- **State charity regulators** — charitable solicitation; registration
- **Donor protection** — state donor privacy laws; FTC rules on fundraising claims
- **Employment** — if you employ staff, normal employment AI rules apply
- **Program compliance** — grantor rules (federal pass-through, foundation restrictions)
- **Data privacy** — CCPA/CPRA, GDPR, HIPAA if health-adjacent, FERPA if education-adjacent
- **Accessibility** — Section 508 (if federally funded), ADA Title III
- **Specific state AI rules** — if your work reaches regulated populations (students, patients, housing applicants)

> **How to use this kit.** A lighter-weight kit for resource-constrained nonprofits. Focuses on the 80% of risk management that most organizations need without the overhead of enterprise frameworks.

---

## 1. The nonprofit reality

Nonprofits are using AI everywhere: grant writing, donor outreach, program delivery, beneficiary services, advocacy, research. Budgets are tight, IT is thin, and "it's a free tool" is a common origin story for AI deployments.

That doesn't mean the rules don't apply. Three things to know:

1. **Donors and grantors expect you to be thoughtful.** AI-produced pitches, mass-fabricated personal stories, or leaked donor data can end relationships fast.
2. **Program participants are often vulnerable.** Beneficiaries in homelessness services, domestic violence support, immigration assistance, or children's programs deserve extra care with AI.
3. **Small doesn't mean exempt.** Employment AI, tenant screening for housing programs, automated eligibility decisions — the laws apply regardless of size.

## 2. Practical scope

Focus your AI governance where risk is highest:

### High risk — extra care required

- Beneficiary-facing AI that makes eligibility / priority decisions
- AI handling protected characteristics (health, immigration status, LGBTQ+, survivor of abuse, etc.)
- AI screening donors or participants
- Automated communications to beneficiaries

### Medium risk — reasonable care

- AI used in grant writing / development
- AI for program outreach and marketing
- AI in HR / hiring
- AI handling donor PII

### Low risk — baseline good practice

- AI for internal productivity (email drafting, brainstorming, summarizing public docs)
- AI for research assistance
- AI for social media content

## 3. The five-thing baseline

Most nonprofits can meet "reasonable" AI governance with five things:

1. **A one-page AI policy** — what tools are OK, what data is red-lined
2. **A short approved tools list**
3. **A training module** (or one hour in the next staff meeting)
4. **A way to report concerns**
5. **A review cadence** (annual at minimum)

This kit includes all of the above in adapted form.

## 4. Beneficiary privacy

- Don't put identifiable beneficiary information into consumer AI tools
- Be especially careful with populations whose status creates real-world risk (undocumented people, abuse survivors, people with HIV, political dissidents in foreign contexts)
- Document data-handling practices; many grantors expect them
- If you provide health services, HIPAA may apply
- If you provide education services, FERPA may apply

## 5. Donor data

- Donor data is often covered by state charitable privacy laws
- Never paste donor lists into public AI tools
- Fundraising AI that generates personalized outreach must not manufacture false personal connections or testimonials
- Compliance with CAN-SPAM, TCPA applies to automated fundraising

## 6. Fundraising communications

- AI-drafted donor letters reviewed for accuracy (numbers, facts, pronouns of real donors)
- AI-generated images of "beneficiaries" must not misrepresent (no stock-like AI renders presented as real people you serve)
- Transparency where a reasonable donor would want to know (AI-drafted major-gift communications probably warrant disclosure in many cases)
- Celebrity / influencer endorsements subject to FTC rules even in nonprofit context

## 7. Grant writing

- AI-assisted grant writing is generally acceptable
- Verify every number, citation, and program claim before submission
- Hallucinated impact figures are a fast path to reputational damage and grantor loss of trust
- Some grantors ask whether AI was used; be prepared to answer honestly

## 8. Hiring

- Small nonprofits often use ATS features with AI — same rules apply
- NYC LL 144, Illinois AIVIA, Colorado, California obligations apply regardless of size
- Board must understand the HR AI posture

## 9. Program delivery AI

- Case management AI, beneficiary chatbots, eligibility automation — treat as high-risk
- Human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions
- Accessibility under ADA
- Accommodation paths for people who can't / don't want to use AI

## 10. Advocacy and content

- AI-generated messaging should be honest about sourcing
- Deepfakes or synthetic imagery of real persons (officials, members of public) create legal risk and movement-reputation risk
- Election-adjacent messaging: state laws and FCC rules apply

## 11. Governance light

A realistic nonprofit governance structure:

- **Board sponsor** — one board member with AI as a portfolio item
- **Staff lead** — ED/CEO or designated operations lead
- **Policy** — one-page, plus the AUP
- **Inventory** — kept in a shared doc or simple spreadsheet
- **Training** — annual, 30 minutes
- **Review** — quarterly ED check-in; annual board update

## 12. Practical readiness path (budget: low)

1. Pick one person as the AI lead
2. Adapt the AI Acceptable Use Policy (consumer-friendly version) and publish to staff
3. Set up an approved tools list
4. Do a one-hour staff briefing
5. Build a simple inventory
6. Add an AI item to the risk register
7. Review annually

## 13. Common traps

- Beneficiary data in consumer AI tools
- AI-generated fundraising content that exaggerates
- Grant applications with hallucinated statistics
- Hiring AI without candidate notice
- "We're a small nonprofit" exemption thinking for HR AI
- AI chatbots serving vulnerable populations without safety guardrails
