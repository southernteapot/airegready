# AI Board Report — How to Fill It Out

Short guide for the AI Governance Lead. The template is in `template.md`.

---

## When

- **Cadence:** quarterly (some companies prefer semi-annual; quarterly is recommended)
- **Lead time:** distribute at least [5 business days] before the board meeting
- **Finalization:** signed off by Executive Sponsor and General Counsel before distribution

## How long should it be

- **Target:** 6–10 pages including appendices
- **Executive summary:** one paragraph — if the board reads nothing else, they should get the shape of the quarter
- **Detail sections:** 2–4 bullets each; appendices for data tables

If you find yourself writing more than 15 pages, you're likely saying too much. The board wants clarity and the two or three things they should worry about.

## What each section should convey

### Executive summary

- What's working, what's changing, what's concerning, what you need from the board
- Board members who skim only this should know whether to probe further

### AI footprint

- Board wants scale, not a catalog
- Call out material additions, retirements, and tier changes
- Surprises (e.g., "shadow AI in finance turned out to be larger than expected") belong here

### Regulatory posture

- One line per material regime
- "Compliant", "in progress", "gap identified" — honest labels
- Link to the evidence pack for board members who want to verify

### Incidents

- Be concrete; redact sensitive details in the main report and offer full write-ups in the appendix or in executive session
- Emphasize what you learned, not what went wrong
- Don't let the board be surprised by an incident they should have been told about earlier

### Program metrics

- Pick 8–10 metrics and keep them stable over time
- Trend arrows matter more than absolute numbers
- Define each metric in a footnote the first time it appears

### Top risks

- Three is the magic number. If you list ten, the board won't remember any.
- Each risk should have an owner and a timeline. "We're watching it" isn't a mitigation.

### Regulatory watch

- Focus on the next 6–12 months
- Explain the *why it matters* to us, not the news coverage

### Third-party / vendor posture

- Call out any vendor risks that could materially affect us (M&A, breach, policy shift)
- Board will want to know if a core vendor is wobbling

### Investments

- Numbers should reconcile to the finance system — if they don't, FP&A will correct you in the room
- Show outcomes, not just spend

### Asks for the board

- Be specific. "Approve budget of $X for Y" or "Ratify new Tier 3 approval for Z."
- Don't overstuff — two or three asks per report is plenty

### Looking ahead

- Anchor the next quarter so the board can follow continuity

## Tone

- **Direct, non-defensive.** Say what happened. Don't soften.
- **Low jargon.** Not every board member is a technologist or a lawyer.
- **Specific over abstract.** "Our resume screener's impact ratio for Black female candidates dropped to 0.72 in August; we retired the tool and restored human-only screening within 48 hours" is better than "we encountered a bias challenge and addressed it."
- **Humble about uncertainty.** If you don't know, say so and when you will.

## Preparation checklist

- [ ] Pull AI System Inventory snapshot
- [ ] Pull incident log and sanitize
- [ ] Collect program metrics with trend direction
- [ ] Review risks with Legal and Executive Sponsor
- [ ] Confirm regulatory watch entries with Legal
- [ ] Draft asks with Executive Sponsor alignment
- [ ] Executive Sponsor and General Counsel review
- [ ] Distribute with lead time

## Executive session

Sensitive items (ongoing incidents, regulator inquiries, personnel matters, litigation risk, M&A-adjacent AI issues) should be reserved for executive session — not the written report. Bring them in an oral update.

## After the meeting

- Capture action items from board feedback
- Update the risk register with anything the board surfaced
- File the approved version in the board record
