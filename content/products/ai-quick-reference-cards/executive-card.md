# AI Quick Reference — Executives

A one-pager for execs navigating AI at [COMPANY NAME]. You set the tone — the rest of the organization follows.

---

## Your five obligations

1. **Be accountable** — AI decisions that affect people, customers, or compliance ultimately answer to the exec team. Know what's in your AI footprint.
2. **Fund the program** — AI governance requires budget for tooling, audits, training, and people. Don't underfund and expect to be safe.
3. **Model the behavior** — if you paste confidential strategy into a public AI tool, everyone else will too. Set the example.
4. **Ask the hard questions** — "What data is in that model? Is that legal where we operate? Did anyone check for bias?"
5. **Be honest externally** — FTC, regulators, and investors now scrutinize AI claims. Overstating AI capabilities is a legal risk.

---

## What your org should have

- [ ] A named AI Governance Lead with authority and budget
- [ ] An AI Review Committee meeting on a regular cadence
- [ ] A current AI System Inventory
- [ ] An AI Acceptable Use Policy that every employee has acknowledged
- [ ] Impact assessments on every high-stakes AI system
- [ ] A bias audit process for people-decisioning AI
- [ ] An incident response plan with AI-specific scenarios
- [ ] Vendor risk assessments with DPAs and BAAs where required
- [ ] Customer disclosures where AI meaningfully affects the experience
- [ ] An annual AI report to the board (see AI Board Report template)

If any of the above is missing, that's where to focus first.

---

## Questions to ask in quarterly reviews

- "How many AI systems are we operating? How many are high-stakes?"
- "Which jurisdictions regulate our AI use, and are we compliant?"
- "What's our shadow AI risk? When did we last look?"
- "What incidents have we had, and what did we learn?"
- "Where is AI creating value — by team, by customer cohort?"
- "Where are we exposed if tomorrow's regulation looks like today's proposal?"
- "Do we have a kill switch for any critical AI system, and has it been tested?"
- "Are our AI vendors still aligned with our security and regulatory posture?"

---

## Red flags

- AI claims in marketing that engineering can't defend
- New product features shipping without the AI Review Committee's sign-off
- "We're small, we don't need to worry about [X law]" without counsel's written opinion
- Shadow AI use tolerated because it's producing results
- Customer complaints about AI that aren't reaching you
- Vendor locked in with no exit plan
- Models in production without post-deployment monitoring
- No named business owner for a system that affects customers

---

## What you should not do

- Claim AI we haven't built
- Promise AI capabilities to investors without engineering's sign-off
- Paste customer data, strategy, or financials into public AI tools
- Approve high-risk AI deployment without an impact assessment
- Assume the vendor's documentation covers us
- Let "move fast" override documented controls
- Hide near-misses from the board

---

## Incident posture

If a serious AI incident happens:

- Your Incident Commander runs the room; you don't
- You're kept informed on SEV-1 and SEV-2
- Critical calls: external communications, regulator notifications, material disclosure decisions
- After the dust settles: make sure the postmortem happens and that action items land

---

## Board / investor talking points (safe language)

- "We use AI in [CATEGORIES] with documented governance and regular audits"
- "Our AI program aligns to [NIST AI RMF / ISO 42001 / EU AI Act / Colorado AI Act] as applicable"
- "We run bias audits on every AI system that makes decisions about people"
- "We monitor AI incidents and publish a quarterly risk report"

Avoid:

- "Our AI is unbiased" (nothing is)
- "Our AI always has a human in the loop" (unless you can prove it, every time)
- "We don't use customer data for training" (unless your contracts and actual practice say so)
