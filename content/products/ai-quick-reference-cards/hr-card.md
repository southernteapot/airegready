## The 5 rules

1. A human is accountable for the work — AI assists
2. Confidential stays confidential — don't paste into unapproved tools
3. Check the output — AI writes plausible wrong answers
4. Be honest about AI involvement when it matters
5. Follow the law — when in doubt, ask

---

## Green / Yellow / Red

- **Green** — Public job market data, public industry benchmarks → any approved tool
- **Yellow** — Internal policies, anonymized compensation data → approved tools only
- **Red** — Candidate resumes, employee files, salary data, medical info, performance reviews → **approved HR tools only**

---

## What you can do

- Draft job descriptions and postings (human review for bias, legal compliance)
- Generate interview question sets
- Summarize interview notes (with AI-disclosure notice, participant consent)
- Draft candidate rejection emails from templates
- Analyze anonymized headcount data
- Translate job postings and candidate communications
- Draft policy updates

---

## What you can't do

- Make final hiring, firing, promotion, pay, or disciplinary decisions based on AI output alone
- Use AI to analyze candidate race, ethnicity, religion, age, disability, gender identity, sexual orientation, or other protected characteristics (directly or by proxy)
- Use AI to analyze micro-expressions, voice tone, or appearance in a way linked to protected classes
- Deploy a new AEDT (automated employment decision tool) without a current independent bias audit
- Skip the 10-business-day candidate notice for NYC-based roles or NYC candidates
- Deny a candidate's accommodation or alternative-selection request without review
- Use AI-generated videos of employees (e.g., for training) without consent

## HR-specific scenarios

**"Can AI rank resumes for me?"** → Only if the tool is approved, has a current independent bias audit (NYC LL 144 where applicable), candidates got proper notice, and a human makes the final call.

**"Can we use AI to draft employee termination letters?"** → Draft yes, with human review. The decision and the final language must be reviewed by HR and, for material matters, Legal.

**"Can we use an AI-scored personality test for candidates?"** → Only if evaluated for bias, disclosed to candidates, subject to accommodation requests, and never used as the sole basis for a decision.

**"Video interview AI — OK?"** → In Illinois, follow AIVIA: notify, explain, obtain consent, destroy on request. In other jurisdictions, similar disclosures are best practice. EU AI Act Annex III treats this as high-risk.

**"Can we monitor employee Slack for sentiment?"** → No. Workplace emotion inference is prohibited under EU AI Act; problematic in many U.S. states. Any workplace monitoring needs Legal + Executive approval.

**"A candidate asked for a human-only review."** → Honor it. Route to a qualified recruiter. Document and track.

**"Can I paste a resume into ChatGPT to rewrite the bullet points?"** → No. The resume contains personal data. Use an approved tool instead.

## Candidate notice (NYC LL 144 summary)

- **10 business days before** AEDT use
- **What the AEDT does, what qualifications it evaluates, what data it uses, retention policy, accommodation contact**
- Link to the bias audit summary

---

## Adverse-action notice (when AI contributed materially)

- Principal reasons in plain language
- Data sources used
- Right to correct data
- Right to appeal to a human
- FCRA disclosures where applicable
