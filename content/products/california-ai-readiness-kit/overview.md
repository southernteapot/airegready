# California AI Readiness Overview

**Regulations covered:**

- **SB 942** — California AI Transparency Act (AI-generated content disclosure / detection tools)
- **AB 2013** — AI Training Data Transparency Act (training data disclosure)
- **SB 53 (TFAIA)** — Transparency in Frontier Artificial Intelligence Act (frontier developer safety framework and incident reporting)
- **SB 1001** — Bot Disclosure Law (requires disclosure that users are interacting with a bot in certain contexts)
- **CCPA/CPRA automated decision-making (ADM) regulations** — privacy-based rules for automated decisionmaking technology
- **AB 2655** — Defending Democracy from Deepfake Deception Act (election deepfakes)
- **AB 2905 / AB 2885** — related definitional and AI-robocall rules

**Enforcement:** California Attorney General (primary), California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) for privacy-adjacent rules; limited private rights of action depending on statute.

> **How to use this kit.** California has no single "AI Act" — it has a patchwork of statutes, each with its own triggers, definitions, and deadlines. This overview helps you navigate the patchwork. Then work through `checklist.md` to build compliance for the laws most likely to affect your company. Pair with the AI Governance Framework and the Customer AI Disclosure Templates.

## 1. Why California matters

California is the largest U.S. state, headquarters to most of the AI industry, and historically the first mover on technology regulation. Its AI rules are often bellwethers for national norms. Companies that want national-scale operations usually treat California compliance as a baseline.

Each California AI law has a narrower scope than Colorado's AI Act or the EU AI Act, but stacked together they touch most AI use cases.

## 2. SB 942 — AI Transparency Act

**Applies to:** Covered providers of generative AI systems that produce image, video, or audio content and have over one million monthly visitors / users in California.

**What it requires (summary):**

- **Manifest disclosure** in AI-generated content when requested — a clearly visible mark that the content is AI-generated
- **Latent disclosure** (machine-readable provenance information) embedded in content whenever technically feasible
- **AI detection tool** provided free to the public to check whether content was generated by the provider's system
- **Contracts** with third parties that use the provider's system must require preservation of the latent disclosure

**Effective:** January 1, 2026

**Compliance angle:** Providers — wire provenance into the output pipeline; publish a detection tool; update third-party contracts. Smaller providers may be below the threshold but should track this as a signal of where larger markets are heading.

## 3. AB 2013 — AI Training Data Transparency

**Applies to:** Developers who make a generative AI system or service publicly available in California (with limited exceptions for certain pre-2022 systems and internal-only systems).

**What it requires:**

- A **public documentation** disclosure posted on the developer's website describing the data used to train the generative AI system
- Documentation covers: sources or owners of datasets; description of how datasets further the purpose of the AI system; number of data points; whether datasets include personal information or aggregate consumer information; whether datasets contain copyrighted, trademarked, or patented material; whether datasets are purchased or licensed; time period during which data was collected; whether data was modified or cleaned; whether synthetic data is used

**Effective:** January 1, 2026, for systems made available on or after January 1, 2022.

**Compliance angle:** Requires you to document what's in your training corpus. For open-source-model users and fine-tuners, inherit what you can from the base model plus document your additions.

## 4. SB 53 — TFAIA (Transparency in Frontier AI Act)

**Applies to:** Frontier AI developers — broadly, companies building very large models (above a compute / performance threshold) or operating in the "frontier" category.

**What it requires (summary):**

- **Safety and security framework** publicly published
- **Reporting** of critical safety incidents to the Office of Emergency Services
- **Whistleblower protections** for employees who raise safety concerns
- **Public statement** about the framework, including catastrophic-risk mitigations

**Compliance angle:** Most small and mid-size companies are not frontier developers. If you are fine-tuning an existing foundation model, you are likely downstream. If you are building foundation models approaching frontier thresholds, legal engagement is essential.

## 5. SB 1001 — Bot Disclosure Law

**Applies to:** Online persons using automated accounts (bots) to communicate with Californians in certain commercial and political contexts.

**What it requires:**

- Clear and conspicuous disclosure that the user is communicating with a bot
- Applies to commerce transactions and influencing electoral votes

**Effective:** Since 2019 — already in force.

**Compliance angle:** If you run sales chatbots or political outreach bots reaching Californians, disclose.

## 6. CCPA / CPRA — Automated Decisionmaking

The California Privacy Protection Agency has promulgated regulations addressing automated decisionmaking technology ("ADMT"). These rules require (summary):

- **Pre-use notice** when ADMT is used for significant decisions or profiling
- **Right to opt out** of ADMT in certain contexts
- **Right to access** meaningful information about the logic, use, and likely outcomes of ADMT
- **Risk assessments** for certain high-risk processing, including use of ADMT for sensitive purposes

Final rule contours have continued to evolve. Track the CPPA's rulemaking for current requirements.

**Compliance angle:** If you use AI to make or materially inform decisions about Californians, your privacy program must extend to ADMT disclosures, opt-outs, and assessments.

## 7. AB 2655 — Election Deepfakes

- Prohibits distribution of materially deceptive audio/visual media relating to candidates within specified pre-election windows
- Requires platforms to implement controls
- Private right of action for candidates and elections officials

**Compliance angle:** Platforms hosting user-generated content operating in California need detection and response processes. Generative content tools should include election-context guardrails.

## 8. Additional California AI-adjacent rules

- **AB 1008** — extends CCPA definitions to cover AI-generated personal information
- **AB 2885** — consolidates the definition of "artificial intelligence" across California law
- **AB 2905** — AI-voice robocalls must disclose the use of AI
- **AB 3030 / SB 1120** — AI in healthcare (disclosures and clinical utilization review)
- **AB 1760 / SB 1047 (vetoed)** — proposals that did not pass but illustrate direction of regulatory interest

## 9. Who this kit is for

- **Gen AI providers** with California-facing consumer volume — SB 942, AB 2013 are likely in scope
- **Deployers of AI in California** — CCPA ADMT rules, SB 1001 bot disclosure, sector rules
- **Platforms** hosting user-generated or AI-generated content — AB 2655 deepfake rules
- **Healthcare operators using AI** — AB 3030 / SB 1120 disclosures
- **Frontier developers** — TFAIA / SB 53 safety framework

## 10. Practical readiness steps

1. **Inventory** every AI system that touches California consumers, employees, or residents
2. **Classify** each system against: SB 942 provider threshold, AB 2013 developer threshold, TFAIA frontier threshold, CCPA ADMT scope, SB 1001 bot, AB 2655 deepfake, sector-specific (AB 3030 healthcare)
3. **Document training data** per AB 2013 for any gen AI you develop / make publicly available
4. **Implement provenance** per SB 942 — manifest and latent marks; stand up a detection tool if in scope
5. **Wire CCPA ADMT disclosures** into consumer-facing flows
6. **Configure bot disclosures** per SB 1001 for commercial and political bots
7. **Build deepfake / synthetic-media labeling** into generation pipelines
8. **Frontier developers:** publish safety framework, establish incident reporting channel, document whistleblower protections
9. **Privacy program extensions:** risk assessments for ADMT, opt-out mechanisms, access request handling
10. **Monitor CPPA rulemaking** and AG enforcement actions for ongoing changes

## 11. Common traps

- **Treating California as one law.** It's seven-plus laws; check each for scope.
- **Threshold complacency.** Usage metrics grow; today's "below threshold" can be next quarter's "in scope."
- **No provenance.** Running generative products without any provenance capability is no longer sustainable in California.
- **CCPA program gap.** Legacy CCPA privacy programs may not cover ADMT — the gap is the violation.
- **Election-window blind spots.** AB 2655 operates on tight pre-election windows; prepare ahead.
