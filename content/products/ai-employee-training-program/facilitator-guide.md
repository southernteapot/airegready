# AI Training — Facilitator Guide

For whoever is delivering this training live — HR lead, AI Governance Lead, or a trained manager.

---

## Preparation

### 1 week before

- [ ] Confirm audience list and RSVPs
- [ ] Update `slide-deck.md` with current approved tools and examples
- [ ] Prepare 1-2 role-relevant real-world examples from the last 12 months (anonymized)
- [ ] Gather links to: policy, approved tools list, vendor request form, incident reporting channel
- [ ] Send calendar invite with agenda and prereads (optional one-page summary)

### 1 day before

- [ ] Test video/audio
- [ ] Print quiz and attestation forms if in-person
- [ ] Reach out to any attendees who've asked to attend with specific questions

### 30 minutes before

- [ ] Arrive early; test display
- [ ] Have the approved tools list open in a tab for live reference

---

## Delivery tips

### Tone

- **Plain-English, not hectoring.** The audience's default reaction is "another compliance training"; turn it into "hey, this is actually useful."
- **Examples over abstractions.** Every major rule should come with a realistic scenario.
- **Admit uncertainty honestly.** If someone asks about a new tool or a new law, "I don't know, let me find out" is better than guessing.
- **No shaming.** "We've all almost done this" beats "don't be the idiot who..."

### Pacing

- Slides 1-5 (framing): 10 minutes total — don't rush, this is where you earn attention
- Slides 6-9 (policy overview): 10 minutes
- Slides 10-23 (rules and scenarios): 15 minutes — this is the meat
- Slides 24-28 (wrap): 5 minutes

If you're running long, cut the role-specific add-ons (they can be done separately).

### Engagement

- Ask the room: "Who has used [TOOL] for work in the last month?" — it's disarming
- Have 2-3 scenario slides where you ask the room what to do, then reveal the answer
- Invite the room to share a near-miss or question (non-punitive)

### Difficult questions

Common ones and short answers:

**"Can I use [free AI tool] for internal work if I don't paste anything confidential?"**
Use the approved tools list. If it's on the list for internal use, yes. If it isn't, request approval — that's the path.

**"What if I think the policy is wrong about [X]?"**
Tell us. The policy changes when it's wrong. Email [AI GOVERNANCE LEAD].

**"Is the policy going to change?"**
Yes. It reviews at least annually and on material changes. We'll re-train you.

**"What about the AI I'm using to write this training material?"**
Good question. If we use AI to draft company communications, we disclose where it would matter to the reader. Same rule as for your work.

**"What happens if I accidentally paste something into the wrong tool?"**
Tell us. Fast. We'll assess scope and decide whether any follow-up (to customer, to regulator) is needed. Honest self-reports are treated very differently from hidden incidents.

### Red flags to escalate

If during training someone mentions:
- A tool being used that's not on the approved list
- A data-handling practice that sounds like it violates the policy
- A decision-making pattern that sounds like it might have compliance implications

Acknowledge it calmly and follow up privately. Don't turn the session into an investigation.

## After the session

### Immediately

- Send a follow-up email with the one-page summary, policy link, approved tools list, and the quiz/attestation form
- Post a recording (if recorded) to [LOCATION]

### Within 3 business days

- Track completion; remind attendees who haven't finished the quiz
- Collect signed attestations in personnel records

### Within 2 weeks

- Review feedback; note questions that came up repeatedly and update the deck
- Flag any emergent issues from the session to the AI Review Committee

---

## Continuous improvement

- Keep a running list of "live session" questions you didn't have a good answer to; bring them to the AI Review Committee
- Refresh the deck quarterly with a new scenario or two
- When a real incident happens, anonymize and incorporate as a teaching example (with approval)
