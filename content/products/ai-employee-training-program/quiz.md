## Question 1

A teammate asks you to help summarize a stack of customer support tickets. The tickets contain customer names, email addresses, and account details. Which of these is the right approach?

A. Paste the tickets into any free AI tool — it's faster.
B. Paste the tickets into an approved AI tool that is authorized for customer data.
C. Paste the tickets into any tool, as long as you delete the chat afterward.
D. Don't use AI at all — only do this manually.

**Correct answer:** B

**Why:** Customer data is in the **red** category. It may only be used in a tool explicitly approved for that data class. Deleting the chat after the fact doesn't reverse the exposure (the vendor may have already used or retained it). Answer D is overly restrictive — approved tools exist for a reason.

## Question 2

You're drafting a customer-facing FAQ for our website with the help of an approved AI tool. Which of the following is required before you publish?

A. Nothing — the AI's output was clear and grammatical.
B. A quick proofread for typos.
C. A human review for accuracy, tone, and any factual claims (including URLs and "studies show" references).
D. A legal review of every word.

**Correct answer:** C

**Why:** AI outputs should not be published externally without a human review focused on accuracy — especially factual claims, links, and quotes. Legal review is valuable in some cases but not required for every piece of AI-assisted content.

## Question 3

A candidate is applying for an open role and we use an AI-assisted resume screener. Which is true?

A. We can reject the candidate based solely on the AI's output, as long as we keep records.
B. We must provide the candidate with advance notice that an AI tool is used and give them a way to request alternative review.
C. We don't need to tell candidates anything because it's an internal tool.
D. Only candidates in NYC need to be notified.

**Correct answer:** B

**Why:** Multiple laws (NYC LL 144, Illinois, Colorado, California, EU) require advance notice and a path for accommodation or alternative selection. A human — not AI alone — must be accountable for the decision. Answer D is a trap: requirements vary but NYC is not the only jurisdiction with obligations.

## Question 4

A. You announce the recording/transcription at the start of the meeting and give attendees a chance to object.
B. You quietly record and transcribe — people usually don't mind.
C. You only need consent if the meeting includes legal counsel.
D. You can record any meeting where at least one party consents.

**Correct answer:** A

**Why:** [COMPANY NAME] requires announcement and consent for AI-assisted meeting recording/transcription. Several states also require all-party consent under wiretap laws; our policy sets a uniform standard.

## Question 5

You discover that a teammate has been pasting proprietary source code into a public AI chatbot for debugging help for several weeks. What should you do?

A. Nothing — it's not your problem.
B. Tell the teammate privately that the policy prohibits this, and report the practice to [AI GOVERNANCE LEAD] so the team can understand scope and remediate.
C. Wait until something bad happens and then escalate.
D. Post about it on a public internal channel so others learn.

**Correct answer:** B

**Why:** Report early and through the right channel. Early reporting lets us assess scope, notify any affected customers if needed, and provide a better tool. Public shaming is counterproductive; waiting for a bad outcome makes it worse.
