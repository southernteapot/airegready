# AI Marketing Claims Review Checklist

**Purpose:** Pre-publish check for any marketing claim that mentions AI. Run before a claim goes on a website, ad, pitch deck, press release, product launch, or investor material.

**Audience:** Marketing, product marketing, brand, legal, PR.

> **Why this matters.** FTC has explicitly flagged AI claims as a priority enforcement area. State AGs are following. "AI-washing" lawsuits by investors and consumers are emerging. A five-minute check before publishing prevents large fines and retractions later.

## Step 1 — What kind of claim is this?

- [ ] **Capability claim** — what the AI does (e.g., "our AI drafts emails in your voice")
- [ ] **Quality claim** — how well it does it (e.g., "unmatched accuracy")
- [ ] **Safety / fairness claim** — assurances about its behavior (e.g., "unbiased AI hiring")
- [ ] **Compliance claim** — regulatory posture (e.g., "EU AI Act compliant")
- [ ] **Comparative claim** — better than alternatives (e.g., "outperforms every competitor")
- [ ] **Endorsement claim** — another party supports or uses it (e.g., "trusted by Fortune 500")
- [ ] **Mechanism claim** — how it works (e.g., "patented AI technology")

Different claim types require different substantiation.

## Step 2 — Is it true?

- [ ] **2.1** The literal statement is accurate
- [ ] **2.2** The implied meaning to a reasonable reader is accurate
- [ ] **2.3** No material qualifications are omitted
- [ ] **2.4** The claim stands alone (doesn't depend on fine print)

If any box isn't checked, revise.

## Step 3 — Is it substantiated?

Specific substantiation by claim type:

### Capability

- [ ] **3.1** The product can do what's claimed for a reasonable user
- [ ] **3.2** Evidence available (demos, tests, customer case studies)
- [ ] **3.3** Limitations noted where material

### Quality / performance

- [ ] **3.4** Specific benchmark or metric cited
- [ ] **3.5** Methodology documented
- [ ] **3.6** Benchmark fairly represents the claim
- [ ] **3.7** Source available for review

### Safety / fairness

- [ ] **3.8** Testing has been done
- [ ] **3.9** Methodology documented
- [ ] **3.10** Results support the claim
- [ ] **3.11** Caveats included where the claim could overstate

### Compliance

- [ ] **3.12** Exact regulation named or "designed to support compliance with" used
- [ ] **3.13** Where a certification exists (SOC 2, ISO, etc.), certification is current and in scope
- [ ] **3.14** Don't claim compliance with a rule that doesn't have a compliance mechanism (e.g., "GDPR-certified" is not a thing)
- [ ] **3.15** Legal has reviewed

### Comparative

- [ ] **3.16** Comparison is fair (same task, same conditions, comparable competitors)
- [ ] **3.17** "Best," "only," "unmatched," "leading" — can we actually defend these?
- [ ] **3.18** If not, soften ("among the strongest," "one of the leading")

### Endorsement / testimonial

- [ ] **3.19** Endorser gave permission
- [ ] **3.20** Endorser actually uses the product (or was properly incentivized disclosure)
- [ ] **3.21** Endorser is real (no AI-generated testimonials — prohibited by FTC)
- [ ] **3.22** Material connections disclosed per FTC Endorsement Guides
- [ ] **3.23** Results reflect typical experience, or disclosure clarifies

### Mechanism

- [ ] **3.24** "Patented" — is there actually a patent? ✓ specific patent number
- [ ] **3.25** "Proprietary" — we actually developed or have an exclusive license
- [ ] **3.26** "AI-powered" — AI is genuinely core, not a bolt-on
- [ ] **3.27** "Machine learning" — we actually use ML (not just rules-based logic)
- [ ] **3.28** "Generative AI" — we actually use generative AI
- [ ] **3.29** Don't use branded terms we don't own

## Step 4 — Specific red-flag words

If the claim uses any of these, double-check:

- [ ] **"Unbiased"** — nothing is. Use "we test for bias" or "we mitigate bias through [METHODS]"
- [ ] **"Guaranteed"** — AI rarely guarantees. Specifics please.
- [ ] **"Hallucination-free"** — false for LLMs. Use "with safeguards against hallucination"
- [ ] **"Autonomous"** — implies capability that may not be true; qualify
- [ ] **"Self-learning"** — usually means "updates periodically"; be precise
- [ ] **"Real-time AI"** — often misused; describe the actual latency
- [ ] **"Military-grade security"** — means nothing; describe actual controls
- [ ] **"FDA-approved"** — only if actually approved; "FDA-cleared" is different
- [ ] **"HIPAA-compliant"** — HIPAA applies to entities; products support compliance
- [ ] **"SOC 2 certified"** — SOC 2 is a report, not a certification; "SOC 2 Type II report available"

## Step 5 — Disclosures

- [ ] **5.1** Material qualifications ("for certain use cases", "under typical conditions") are included
- [ ] **5.2** Footnotes / disclaimers are proximate to the claim, not hidden
- [ ] **5.3** Disclaimers are readable (not 8pt gray text)
- [ ] **5.4** Testimonials include disclosure language if compensated

## Step 6 — Audience-specific considerations

### Regulated audiences

- [ ] **6.1** Healthcare — no unsupported medical claims; SaMD considerations; HIPAA care
- [ ] **6.2** Financial services — no unsupported financial/performance claims; disclosure of risk
- [ ] **6.3** Insurance — rate/pricing claims defensible under state DOI scrutiny
- [ ] **6.4** Education — student-outcome claims defensible
- [ ] **6.5** Government — FAR / small business / accessibility claims true

### Investor audience

- [ ] **6.6** Claims in investor materials — same substantiation standard; plus securities laws
- [ ] **6.7** Disclaimer about forward-looking statements

### Political / election

- [ ] **6.8** Claims about political candidates — extra caution; consult legal

## Step 7 — Legal review trigger

Legal review required if the claim:

- [ ] Involves compliance (sets an enforceable expectation)
- [ ] Involves safety (creates duty-of-care exposure)
- [ ] Is in a regulated industry
- [ ] Is comparative with named competitors
- [ ] Is in investor materials
- [ ] Involves IP (patents, trademarks)
- [ ] Could be seen as AI-washing

## If a claim fails review

1. Revise to be more precise and supported
2. Soften where substantiation is weak
3. Remove where unsupportable
4. Document the review and decision

## If a published claim turns out to be wrong

1. Correct promptly on the same channel
2. Update supporting substantiation or retire the claim
3. Log the incident per AI Complaint Handling Procedure
4. Consider notifying affected customers if material
5. Post-mortem: why did this get through?
