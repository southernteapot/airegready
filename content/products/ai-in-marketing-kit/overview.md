# AI in Marketing — Compliance Kit Overview

**Regulatory landscape:**

- **FTC Act Section 5** — unfair or deceptive acts or practices; AI-washing enforcement
- **FTC Endorsement Guides** — disclosure of material connections; rules for AI-generated endorsements
- **FTC Rule on Government and Business Impersonation**
- **TCPA** — automated calls, texts, including AI voice
- **CAN-SPAM** — email advertising
- **State consumer protection laws** — often broader than FTC
- **Truth-in-advertising** laws by state
- **California** — SB 942 AI content transparency; SB 1001 bot disclosure; AB 2655 election deepfakes
- **Texas TRAIGA** — prohibited manipulation and deceptive uses
- **EU AI Act** — Art 50 synthetic media labeling; emotion/manipulation prohibitions
- **GDPR / CCPA** — personal data processing for marketing AI
- **Copyright and trademark** — AI-generated content IP issues
- **Right of publicity / likeness rights** — state laws protect use of real person's likeness

> **How to use this kit.** For in-house marketing teams, agencies, influencer-marketing platforms, ad-tech vendors, brand managers, and creative studios. Pair with AI Acceptable Use Policy, Customer AI Disclosure Templates, and AI Marketing Claims Review Checklist.

## 1. Why marketing AI is different

Marketing is where AI has moved fastest into production, which means:

- **FTC has been explicit and active** about AI claims, endorsements, and deceptive uses
- **Synthetic content** (images, voice, video) is creating novel legal questions (right of publicity, deepfakes, disclosure)
- **Endorsements and influencer content** produced with AI need transparency
- **State laws** on political ads, election deepfakes, and content transparency are multiplying
- **Advertising claims** about AI itself are a new class of deception risk ("AI-powered," "patented AI")

## 2. Top marketing AI risks

1. **AI-washing** — overstating AI capabilities of a product
2. **Fake endorsements / testimonials** — generated or altered by AI
3. **Deepfakes and synthetic likenesses** — using a real person's likeness or voice without consent
4. **AI-generated content that infringes IP** — copies protected works or trademarks
5. **Disparate-impact targeting** — AI lookalike models that entrench bias
6. **Deceptive chatbots / voice agents** — undisclosed, pushy, or misleading
7. **Non-compliant pricing personalization** — dynamic pricing that runs into anti-discrimination or unfair-practices rules
8. **TCPA violations** — AI voice messages without consent

## 3. FTC guidance summary

- **AI claims** must be truthful, non-deceptive, and substantiated. Saying "AI-powered" when the product uses simple heuristics is deceptive.
- **Endorsements** — FTC Endorsement Guides require disclosure of material connections; AI-generated "endorsements" or fake users are deceptive per se.
- **Synthetic testimonials** — treat as you would any fabricated testimonial (prohibited).
- **Operator of AI** bears responsibility for what the AI says. "The AI did it" is not a defense.
- **FTC has pursued** cases against companies making unsupported AI capability claims.

## 4. Endorsements and influencers

- Influencers using AI tools to generate content must still disclose material connections
- Brands compensating influencers whose content is AI-assisted share responsibility
- Affiliate marketing AI (e.g., AI-assisted review aggregation) must not fabricate or manipulate

## 5. Synthetic media (images, voice, video)

- **Disclosure** where required by law (CA SB 942, EU AI Act Art 50, NY election laws, TX TRAIGA)
- **Consent** from any identifiable person used
- **Machine-readable provenance** (C2PA, watermarks) where technically feasible
- **Deepfakes of public figures** — election windows carry special rules; reputational and legal risk year-round

## 6. Right of publicity and likeness

- State laws protect living persons' right to control commercial use of their name, likeness, voice
- Tennessee ELVIS Act protects voice against unauthorized AI reproduction
- Estates retain rights post-mortem in many states (CA, TN, others)
- Voice cloning of real people without consent is a fast path to litigation

## 7. Copyright and trademark

- AI-generated content may or may not be copyrightable (largely not, absent significant human authorship — U.S. Copyright Office)
- Using AI trained on copyrighted works can create derivative-work disputes
- Trademark infringement rules still apply — don't let AI generate competitor logos
- Document your content pipeline so you can support fair use or license claims

## 8. Targeting and personalization

- AI-driven audience targeting must avoid protected-class segmentation that violates fair housing, credit, or employment ad rules
- Dynamic pricing personalization can implicate anti-discrimination laws
- Profiling rules under GDPR, CCPA/CPRA ADMT apply to marketing AI
- Sensitive inferences (health, political, religious) require legal basis and often consent

## 9. TCPA and CAN-SPAM

- AI voice robocalls are regulated (TCPA + state AI voice laws like CA AB 2905)
- Automated texts need prior consent
- Email marketing using AI generation must still comply with CAN-SPAM (honest subject lines, unsubscribe, header info)

## 10. Bot disclosure

- California SB 1001 — commercial/political bot disclosure
- EU AI Act Art 50 — chatbots must disclose AI nature unless obvious
- Texas TRAIGA — consumer disclosure in certain contexts
- FTC treats undisclosed AI agents as potentially deceptive

## 11. Practical readiness path

1. **Inventory** of marketing AI tools
2. **Claims review** process for any AI-related marketing claim
3. **Endorsements policy** for influencers, testimonials, case studies
4. **Synthetic media policy** — consent, disclosure, provenance
5. **Right-of-publicity screen** for any content featuring real people
6. **Targeting review** — protected-class segmentation audit
7. **Bot disclosures** wired into chatbots, voice agents, automated emails
8. **TCPA / CAN-SPAM** compliance wired into outreach
9. **Content pipeline documentation** to support IP defenses
10. **Training** for marketing, creative, and agency partners

## 12. Common traps

- Claiming "AI-powered" when the product uses rules
- Letting AI generate reviews or testimonials
- Using a celebrity's voice/likeness without consent
- Using AI to write personalized financial, medical, or legal advice at scale
- Deepfake political ads in regulated windows
- Targeting ads in protected categories with lookalikes trained on protected-class patterns
- Chatbots claiming to be human when asked
